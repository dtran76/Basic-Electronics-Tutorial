Breadboards</span></h1><p class="c5"><span class="c9">We&rsquo;ll be building all of our circuits on something called a breadboard. A breadboard, sometimes called a protoboard, is a tool used by engineers and hobbyists alike to prototype their circuit designs. The name &ldquo;breadboard&rdquo; comes from when electronics would be prototyped on an actual piece of wood, i.e. a cutting board for slicing bread. However, since their introduction in the &lsquo;70s, the term now refers to the &ldquo;solderless breadboard&rdquo; variety, the kind we&rsquo;ll be using in this guide. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c6"><span style="overflow: hidden; display: inline-block; margin: 0.00px 0.00px; border: 0.00px solid #000000; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); width: 256.63px; height: 207.97px;"><img alt="" src="images/image5.png" style="width: 333.62px; height: 222.40px; margin-left: -32.08px; margin-top: -6.13px; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px);" title=""></span></p><p class="c6 c8"><span class="c18"></span></p><p class="c6"><span class="c28">Figure 1.1</span><span class="c9">. A typical solderless breadboard.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Solderless breadboards have many features that make them extremely convenient for prototyping electronics, some of the biggest of these being that:</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><ul class="c39 lst-kix_1hy4e1w3mw98-0 start"><li class="c5 c20"><span class="c9">the breadboard comes pre-made with many connections, and</span></li><li class="c5 c20"><span class="c9">any additional connections we make are non-permanent.</span></li></ul><h2 class="c7" id="h.x2ucl0nmdm7t"><span class="c26 c17">1.1 Pre-made connections</span></h2><p class="c5"><span>To illustrate this first point, see </span><span class="c28">Figure 1.2</span><span class="c9">. Underneath the plastic top of the breadboard are strips of metal that connect the holes. Since many of the holes are already pre-connected, this eliminates the need to connect our components together ourselves with wire. Breadboards come in many sizes, but the way the holes are connected is the same for all of them.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">First, look to the sides of the breadboard to the areas marked with a plus and minus sign. Running down either length of the breadboard will be a set of red and blue&mdash;or red and black&mdash; lines. These strips are called the power rails and, as the name suggests, is typically where power and ground from a power source are connected. </span></p><p class="c5"><span>The holes on the power rail are typically separated into groups of five with a small gap in between each group. All of the holes on the red side are connected, even across the gaps, and likewise, the holes on the blue side are connected. However, on some breadboards, this connection is broken halfway along the power rail, as shown in </span><span class="c28">Figure 1.3</span><span class="c9">. This is indicated by the red and blue lines being broken and by a gap that is noticeably larger than the normal, connected gaps. If using this kind of breadboard, one can simply bridge the gap with wires.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c6"><span style="overflow: hidden; display: inline-block; margin: 0.00px 0.00px; border: 0.00px solid #000000; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); width: 422.00px; height: 273.00px;"><img alt="" src="images/image9.png" style="width: 422.00px; height: 273.00px; margin-left: 0.00px; margin-top: 0.00px; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px);" title=""></span></p><p class="c6 c8"><span class="c18"></span></p><p class="c6"><span class="c28">Figure 1.2</span><span class="c9">. Breadboard with metal connections highlighted in green. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c6"><span style="overflow: hidden; display: inline-block; margin: 0.00px 0.00px; border: 0.00px solid #000000; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); width: 624.00px; height: 214.88px;"><img alt="" src="images/image10.jpg" style="width: 624.00px; height: 415.85px; margin-left: 0.00px; margin-top: -94.85px; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px);" title=""></span></p><p class="c6"><span class="c28">Figure 1.3.</span><span class="c9">&nbsp;A breadboard with split power rails. The connections are shown in green.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Now, look at the holes in the center of the breadboard. The holes on each row are all connected to each other, but not across the center gap. This splits each row into two groups of five connected holes, called terminal strips. The terminal strips are where components are typically connected. </span></p><h2 class="c7" id="h.gtsuegm7ntqu"><span class="c17 c26">1.2 Non-permanent connections</span></h2><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Another big reason why breadboards are used is because all of the connections that we add are non-permanent. </span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Inside each hole is a metal clamp that attaches to anything we insert, connecting it to the metal underneath. To make a connection on the breadboard, simply insert a wire or component leg into a hole. If, at any point, we discover a mistake in wiring or just change our minds on something, changing the connections is as easy as unplugging the component and plugging it in elsewhere. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Since the connections are non-permanent, there is also the additional benefit that any circuit built on a breadboard can be completely unassembled, and the components and breadboard can be reused for other projects.</span></p><h1 class="c31 c27" id="h.by6h6tsdd62q"><span class="c32 c19">2. The circuit schematic is shown in </span><span class="c28">Figure 2.1</span><span class="c9">.</span></p><p class="c6"><span style="overflow: hidden; display: inline-block; margin: 0.00px 0.00px; border: 0.00px solid #000000; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); width: 136.97px; height: 221.62px;"><img alt="" src="images/image15.png" style="width: 136.97px; height: 221.62px; The &ldquo;light-emitting&rdquo; portion is fairly self-explanatory, but what does &ldquo;diode&rdquo; mean? </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">A diode is an electrical device that only allows current to flow in one direction. While an oversimplification, this definition is more than suitable for the purposes of this guide. In an analogy to plumbing, think of a diode as a one-way valve. These types of valves allow water to flow in one direction, but they prevent any backflow into the pipe. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c6"><span style="overflow: hidden; display: inline-block; margin: 0.00px 0.00px; border: 0.00px solid #000000; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); width: 99.18px; height: 279.50px;"><img alt="" src="images/image21.png" style="width: 99.18px; height: 279.50px; margin-left: 0.00px; margin-top: 0.00px; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px);" title=""></span></p><p class="c6 c8"><span class="c18"></span></p><p class="c6"><span class="c28">Figure 2.2.</span><span>&nbsp;A blue 5 mm LED.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Diodes behave in the same way with electric current. In one direction, they have very little resistance and behave like a short circuit, i.e. as if it were just a piece of wire. In the other direction, they have very high resistance and behave like an open circuit, i.e. as if there were no wire there and the circuit was not completed. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span>The symbol for a diode is shown in </span><span class="c28">Figure 2.3</span><span class="c9">. In this orientation, current can flow from left to right, but not right to left. Think of the triangle as an arrow which points in the direction that current can flow. The bar at the end of the arrow can then be thought of like a barrier that prevents current from flowing back into the arrow. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c6"><span style="overflow: hidden; display: inline-block; margin: 0.00px 0.00px; border: 0.00px solid #000000; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); width: 337.00px; height: 105.14px;"><img alt="" src="images/image12.png" style="width: 500.69px; height: 213.55px; margin-left: -81.04px; margin-top: -55.64px; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px);" title=""></span></p><p class="c6 c8"><span class="c18"></span></p><p class="c6"><span class="c28">Figure 2.3.</span><span>&nbsp;Diode circuit symbol.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span>An LED, like the name suggests, is simply a special type of diode that emits light while conducting. Notice how the legs of the diode are different lengths. The longer leg is the &ldquo;positive&rdquo; side (anode) which corresponds to the left side in </span><span class="c28">Figure 2.3</span><span class="c9">, and the shorter leg is the &ldquo;negative&rdquo; side (cathode) which corresponds to the right side. The negative side is also marked by a flat notch on the LED itself. </span></p><h2 class="c7" id="h.w93anivqrvn9"><span class="c17">2.2 Building the circuit</span></h2><p class="c5"><span class="c9">First, plug the LED in anywhere on the breadboard. The legs of the LED shouldn&rsquo;t be connected together, so have them be on seperate terminal strips (rows). Keep note of which side is positive and negative.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c6"><span style="overflow: hidden; display: inline-block; margin: 0.00px 0.00px; border: 0.00px solid #000000; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); width: 336.50px; height: 217.39px;"><img alt="" src="images/image1.png" style="width: 336.50px; height: 217.39px; margin-left: 0.00px; margin-top: 0.00px; transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px); -webkit-transform: rotate(0.00rad) translateZ(0px);" title=""></span></p><p class="c6 c8"><span class="c18"></span></p><p class="c6"><span class="c28">Figure 2.4.</span><span>&nbsp;LED inserted. The positive side is on the right.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">When an LED conducts, it will have zero resistance. Using Ohm&rsquo;s Law, we can see that the current will be infinite. A real LED will still have some small level of resistance, so the current won&rsquo;t be infinite, but it will still be enough to break the LED. To solve this, we need to add a current-limiting resistor. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span>Add a 1 k&#8486; resistor in series with the LED by inserting one leg in the same terminal strip as the positive leg of the LED. This means that the collector of one shares the same voltage as the base of the other.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">When one of the transistors turns on, the resistance between its collector and emitter drops to close to zero, which according to Ohm&rsquo;s Law, means that the voltage should also drop close to zero. This voltage is the same as the base voltage as the other transistor. The only way we can really know which one of these states it gets stuck in is to build the circuit and power it on. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Once powered on, the system is essentially stuck in this state unless an external influence was to be applied (ex. removing the battery, adding a connection, removing a connection, etc.). The transistor only conducts when there is a high enough voltage at the base, so to turn it off, we need to pull the base voltage to zero, which we can do by connecting it to ground (the negative power rail).</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">Try this on your own breadboard. Again, capacitors are short circuits to sudden changes in voltage, so the voltage on the other side of the capacitor, i.e. the base voltage of the other transistor, will also drop, keeping the other transistor off. So far, everything is just like the bistable system. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">After the system enters this first state, the capacitor on the top side will begin to charge through the 10 k&#8486; resistor. This slowly raises the voltage on the negative end of the capacitor (the side connected to the base of the bottom transistor), and eventually, this voltage will be high enough to turn on the bottom transistor. Current can now flow on the bottom side, turning on the bottom LED and discharging the bottom capacitor.</span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">When the bottom transistor turns on, its collector voltage drops, which also drops the voltage on the other side of the bottom capacitor, which is the same as the base voltage of the top transistor. This turns the top transistor off, switching the system to its second state. </span></p><p class="c3"><span class="c9"></span></p><p class="c5"><span class="c9">From here, the cycle repeats itself and continues indefinitely. This is where the &ldquo;astable oscillator&rdquo; name comes from: &ldquo;astable&rdquo; because there is no state that it gets &ldquo;stuck&rdquo; in forever, and &ldquo;oscillator&rdquo; because it switches between two states.</span></p><h1 class="c27 c31" id="h.w3nr3196hwe8"><span class="c19">Conclusion</span></h1><p class="c5"><span class="c9">With the astable oscillator complete, hopefully you&rsquo;ve learned something along the way, and most importantly, have had fun doing so. Many of the descriptions in this guide have been a vast simplification, but with this baseline knowledge, you should know enough to start learning more on your own.
